Digital Nomad Survival Guide デジタルノマド サバイバルガイド

Practical tips for working remotely in Tokyo. Tested by nomads, verified at 3AM.

📡
Pocket WiFi vs. SIM
Rent a pocket WiFi at the airport (¥500–900/day) for multi-device coverage. If you only need phone data, grab an eSIM from Ubigi or Airalo before landing. Unlimited data SIMs run ¥3,000–5,000 for 30 days. Always have a backup — café WiFi can drop during peak hours.
🔌
Power Adapter Reality
Japan uses Type A plugs (same as US/Canada). Voltage is 100V — most modern laptop chargers handle 100–240V automatically. Check your charger's label. No adapter needed for US plugs. EU/UK travelers need a simple adapter, not a converter. Carry a small power strip — outlets in cafés are precious.
🕐
Time Zone Hacking
Tokyo is JST (UTC+9). If your clients are in the US, your overlap window is 7–10AM JST (ET evening). Europe overlap: 4–7PM JST. Use jet lag to your advantage — wake up at 4AM, crush deep work before Tokyo opens. Many manga cafés are 24h if you need a 3AM workspace.
💴
Cash Still Matters
Many smaller cafés are cash-only. 7-Eleven and Family Mart ATMs accept international cards with no issues. Withdraw ¥10,000–20,000 at a time. IC cards (Suica/PASMO) work at chain cafés, convenience stores, and trains. Load them at any station.
🤫
Café Etiquette
Phone calls in cafés are a hard no in Japan. Step outside or use a phone booth. Many coworking spaces have designated phone areas. Don't spread across multiple seats during peak hours. Order at least one drink per 2 hours as a courtesy. Some kissaten (traditional cafés) frown on laptops — read the room.
🏠
Accommodation for Nomads
Monthly apartments via Sakura House or Weekly Mansion start at ¥80,000/month. Hostels with coworking areas (like UNPLAN Shinjuku) offer ¥3,000–5,000/night. AirBnb is legal in Japan but regulated — look for licensed listings. Stay near a JR Yamanote Line station for easy café-hopping access.
🚃
Getting Between Spots
The Yamanote Line connects most café areas in this guide. Average commute between areas: 10–20 minutes. A PASMO/Suica card auto-charges and works everywhere. Google Maps transit directions are extremely accurate in Tokyo — trust them. Last train is around 12:00–12:30AM. After that: taxi, night bus, or 24h café.
🍙
Fuel Your Work Session
Convenience store onigiri (¥120–200) is the perfect desk snack. Family Mart's iced coffee (¥150) rivals most cafés. Bento boxes at any konbini run ¥400–700. For a proper lunch break, look for teishoku (set meal) restaurants near your café — typically ¥800–1,200 for a complete meal with rice, soup, and sides.
🔋
Battery Emergency Kit
Rent a mobile battery from ChargeSPOT (red stations in every convenience store and station). ¥300 for the first hour, ¥150/hr after. Return at any ChargeSPOT station. Keep a 10,000mAh power bank as permanent EDC. Most manga/internet cafés provide USB charging at every booth.
🗣️
Language Barrier Tips
Google Translate's camera mode reads Japanese menus and signs in real-time. Learn basics: sumimasen (excuse me), WiFi arimasu ka? (is there WiFi?), kochira de ii desu ka? (is it OK to sit here?). Most chain cafés have English menus. DeepL works better than Google for longer text translation.
📱
Essential Apps
Japan Transit (train routes), Google Maps (everything else), Tabelog (restaurant ratings — the Japanese Yelp), PayPay (mobile payments accepted widely), LINE (messaging — Japan's WhatsApp). Download offline maps before arriving. Suica app works on iPhone — add it to Apple Wallet.
🌙
Jet Lag Work Strategy
Days 1–3: Embrace the early wake-ups. Hit a 24h café or manga kissa at 4AM for distraction-free deep work. Days 4–7: Your body adjusts — shift to normal hours but keep an early morning routine. Use afternoon slumps for admin tasks, meetings, or exploration. Evening: wind down at a bar with WiFi — some izakaya have outlets.
