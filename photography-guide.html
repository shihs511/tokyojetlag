#01

Shibuya Scramble Crossing
渋谷スクランブル交差点
Shibuya

The world's busiest pedestrian crossing. Shoot from the Shibuya Sky observation deck for the iconic aerial view, or from Starbucks 2F for a mid-level perspective. Street-level long exposures at night create stunning light trails from umbrellas and phone screens.

📐
Best Angle
Elevated 45° from Shibuya Sky or Mag's Park rooftop
🕐
Best Time
Blue hour (17:30–18:15 winter) / Night after 20:00
👥
Crowd Level
Very High
📷
Tripod
Not at crossing; OK at Sky deck
ISO 400–1600
f/ 2.8–5.6
SS 1/60–2s (trails)
FL 16–35mm
Pro Tip

Time your visit during rain for dramatic reflections on wet asphalt. A 24mm lens captures the full intersection chaos from the Tsutaya Starbucks window.

Seasonal Photography Guide

Tokyo transforms through the seasons—each offers unique light, colors, and photographic opportunities

Spring

March–May

Cherry Blossoms (Sakura)

  • Peak bloom typically March 25–April 15 in central Tokyo
  • Chidoriguchi at Shinjuku Gyoen: massive 500+ specimens, day and night illuminations
  • Ueno Park: 1,200+ trees, best before 10 AM for crowd avoidance
  • Shoot at golden hour for warm tones; overcast skies also work well for detail
  • Use faster shutter speeds (1/250s+) to freeze petal rain when wind picks up
  • Aperture f/4–f/8 keeps foreground and sky in focus with blossoms
  • Night shoots: f/2.8 at ISO 3200–6400 for illuminated trees without flash

Wisteria & Azaleas

  • Wisteria Festival at Asahi Shrine: mid–late April, purple cascades perfect for bokeh
  • Tsutsuji (azalea) Festival at Ota Castle Ruins: mid-April to early May
  • Shoot wisteria clusters with shallow depth of field (f/1.4–f/2) to isolate colors
  • Azaleas pair beautifully with traditional temple architecture

Summer

June–August

Fireworks & Festivals (Hanabi)

  • Sumida River Festival: late July, 20,000+ fireworks, arrive by 4 PM for location
  • Omagatoki Festival: mid-August along Shinjuku riverside, fireworks reflecting on water
  • Shutter speed 2–4 seconds to capture burst trails, aperture f/5.6–f/8
  • Use manual mode, disable flash, shoot in RAW for post-processing flexibility
  • Tripod essential; position before dusk for foreground elements (rivers, crowds)
  • ISO 100–400 to avoid noise in night sky
  • Frame fireworks off-center for compositional interest

Hydrangeas (Ajisai) & Fireflies

  • Sumo Hall hydrangea garden (June) and Asakusa Temple gardens peak mid-June
  • Fireflies at Meiji Shrine forest: mid-June, shoot at f/2.8 ISO 1600, 1-second exposures
  • Saturated blues and purples from hydrangeas; shoot before rain washes them clean
  • Obon Festival (mid-August): lanterns, traditional dress, street processions—vivid color opportunities

Autumn

September–November

Momiji (Maple Leaves)

  • Peak color mid-November; Rikugien Garden and Koishikawa Korakuen are premier spots
  • Rikugien: 650+ maples, night illuminations late October–November
  • Shoot underexposed by 0.3–0.7 stops to saturate reds and oranges
  • Backlighting at sunrise creates glowing leaves (f/2.8–f/4, fast shutter to avoid blown highlights)
  • Close-up macro shots reveal vein detail; shoot f/5.6–f/11 for depth of field
  • Wet leaves after rain photograph darker, more saturated; light mist diffuses morning sun beautifully

Ginkgo & Cosmos Flowers

  • Ginkgo avenue at Meiji Shrine (mid-late November): golden tunnel of trees
  • Cosmos flowers at Shinjuku Gyoen and various parks (September–October)
  • Ginkgo: shoot against blue sky to maximize golden color contrast
  • Harvest festivals: Autumnal food markets, traditional dress, temple grounds

Winter

December–February

Illuminations & Snow

  • Roppongi Hills Artelligent Christmas (November–December): 1.5 million LEDs, best 6–9 PM
  • Shinjuku festive lights and shopping district illuminations December 1–January 15
  • Snow is rare but dramatic; Mount Fuji with fresh snow (best January–early February)
  • Illumination shooting: f/2–f/2.8, ISO 1600–3200, 1/30–1/60 shutter speed
  • RAW mode captures color detail in blown LED highlights for post-processing recovery
  • White balance set to tungsten (3200K) for warm, saturated light effects
  • Tripod crucial for stability; use mirror lock-up or electronic shutter to minimize vibration

New Year & Onsen Steam

  • Meiji Shrine New Year celebration (Jan 1–3): crowds, traditional dress, shrine grounds bathed in morning light
  • Gundam Base at odaiba: winter illuminations through January
  • Onsen steam photographs: shoot at blue hour with steam backlit, f/2.8–f/4, ISO 800–1200
  • Clear winter air and bright sunlight ideal for sharp daytime architecture photography

Gear & Equipment Tips

Optimize your gear for Tokyo's unique lighting and urban environments

Composition Rules

Use the rule of thirds grid overlay (enable in Camera Settings). Position neon signs, reflections, and architecture intersecting grid lines rather than centered. Leading lines from streets and train tracks guide the viewer's eye into the frame.

Night Mode Mastery

Hold completely still (use a makeshift tripod with a book or bag) for 3–5 seconds in iPhone Night Mode. Avoid moving during exposure. For Android, use Google Pixel's Night Sight at ISO 3200+ with longer exposures to capture neon without noise.

Portrait Mode for Bokeh

Use Portrait mode to isolate neon signs and street scenes from busy backgrounds. The simulated bokeh works best at 2–3 meters distance. Avoid extreme angles; shoot at eye level for most natural results.

HDR and Dynamic Range

Enable HDR for high-contrast daytime scenes (bright sky, dark street), but disable it for neon photography to preserve color saturation and detail. Shoot in RAW when your phone supports it (Google Pixel, iPhone 12+) for editing flexibility.

Video Stabilization

Enable Cinematic Mode (iPhone) or Video Stabilization (Android) for smooth handheld walking footage through neon streets. 4K 30fps is optimal for low light; avoid 60fps in night scenes due to shorter shutter times.

Essential Editing Apps

Lightroom Mobile: adjust exposure, contrast, and color temperature non-destructively. Snapseed: apply selective edits to individual areas. VSCO: preset filters tuned for urban/neon aesthetics. Cinemagraph Pro: create animated GIFs of moving lights.

Ultra-Wide Lens (14–24mm)

Captures sweeping vistas of Tokyo skyline and expansive neon-lit streets. Fujifilm XF 16mm f/1.4, Sony FE 20mm f/1.8, or Canon RF 14–35mm f/4L offer excellent corner sharpness. Beware of distortion on buildings; correct in post.

Standard Fast Prime (35–50mm)

Sony 50mm f/1.8 or Fujifilm 35mm f/1.4 excel for street photography and architectural detail. Fixed focal lengths force compositional discipline. f/1.4–f/1.8 ideal for isolating subjects in busy streets and neon-lit nights.

Telephoto Zoom (70–200mm)

Sony FE 70–200mm f/2.8 G Master allows compression of neon signs and Tokyo Tower with surrounding lights. Excellent for wildlife and detail shots from street level. Aperture f/2.8 maintains subject isolation in night scenes.

Filter Kit Essentials

ND Filter: 2–4 stop for long exposures of flowing traffic at night (1–4 second shutter). Polarizing Filter: reduces glare on glass storefronts and wet streets, increases neon saturation. GND Filter: balances bright sky with darker foreground in dawn/dusk shooting.

Battery & Power Management

Tokyo weather alternates between high humidity and cool temps—both drain batteries 20% faster. Carry 3–4 spare batteries per 8-hour shoot. Peak season shoots (sakura, illuminations) run 12+ hours; bring USB-C power bank and in-camera charging if supported.

Sensor Cleaning & Maintenance

Tokyo's humidity and urban dust accumulate quickly. Clean sensor weekly during peak season. Carry rocket blower and sensor swabs. Avoid changing lenses in rain or near puddles. Store gear in sealed waterproof bag with silica packets overnight.

Film Stock Selection

Kodak Portra 400: warm, forgiving skin tones, excellent for street scenes and people; saturation handles neon beautifully. Fujifilm Velvia 50: ultra-saturated, ideal for cherry blossoms and vibrant street details. Kodak Gold 200: retro aesthetic that complements Tokyo neon perfectly.

Push Processing for Neon

Shoot ISO 400 film at ISO 1600 (push 2 stops) to capture dim neon without underexposure. Push processing darkens shadows slightly and increases contrast—ideal for night scenes. Confirm with lab beforehand; Fujifilm Tokyo labs offer same-day push processing.

Where to Buy Film in Tokyo

Yodobashi Camera (Shinjuku): massive selection, competitive pricing, same-day film development available. Bic Camera (Ginza): Kodak, Fujifilm, and Ilford stocks. Local indie shops in Shibuya and Shimokitazawa stock rare and expired film stocks at premium prices.

Film Development Labs

Fujifilm Print Service Centers: standard C-41 and E-6 processing in 1 hour. Yodobashi Labs: bulk discount for multiple rolls. Specialty labs like Tlab in Harajuku offer push/pull processing and contact sheets same-day. Budget ¥800–1,200 per roll development.

Cold Storage & Climate

Tokyo summer humidity (70–85%) accelerates film color shift. Refrigerate unused film at 4°C (convenience stores have coin lockers nearby). Avoid opening film canisters in humid air; load cameras in cool, dry locations. Process film within 1–2 weeks of exposure for color accuracy.

Manual Focus Considerations

Most film cameras require manual focus; practice zone focusing (pre-focusing at expected subject distance) before street shooting. Bright Tokyo daytime allows f/5.6–f/8 apertures with shutter speeds 1/250–1/500, giving generous depth of field for street work without precise focus.
