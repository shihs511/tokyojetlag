Bathing Etiquette 入浴マナー

Master these essentials before your first soak. Respect the water, respect the culture.

1. Remove Shoes

靴を脱ぐ（くつをぬぐ）

Leave shoes in the getabako (shoe locker) at the entrance. Many facilities provide slippers for indoor use. Never wear outdoor shoes beyond the entrance.

2. Get Fully Undressed

全裸になる（ぜんらになる）

In the changing room (脱衣所), remove all clothing and place in a locker or basket. You will enter the bathing area completely nude — swimsuits are not allowed.

3. Wash Thoroughly First

体を洗う（からだをあらう）

Sit on the low stool at a washing station (洗い場). Use soap and shampoo to scrub your entire body and rinse completely before entering any bath.

4. Keep Towel Out of Water

タオルを湯に入れない

Your small towel (手拭い) must never touch the bath water. Fold it and place it on your head or on the edge of the bath.

5. Enter Slowly & Quietly

静かに入る（しずかにはいる）

Lower yourself into the water slowly. Never jump, splash, or make excessive noise. Onsen is a place of tranquility and meditation.

6. No Swimming or Diving

泳がない（およがない）

Baths are for soaking, not swimming. Keep your movements calm. Submerge your body but consider keeping long hair tied up and out of the water.

7. Tattoo Policy Check

入れ墨確認（いれずみかくにん）

Many onsen still prohibit tattoos (入れ墨). Some offer cover-up stickers or private baths. Always check beforehand — we've marked tattoo-friendly spots in this guide.

8. Hydrate After

水分補給（すいぶんほきゅう）

After bathing, drink water or the classic post-onsen milk (牛乳) from the vending machines. Hot baths dehydrate you — especially important when jet-lagged.

Jet Lag Recovery 時差ボケ解消法

How to use Tokyo's bathing culture to reset your body clock and beat jet lag.

Day 1 — Arrival Evening (18:00–21:00)

Warm Soak to Wind Down

After landing, resist napping. Instead, visit a neighborhood sento around 7–8 PM. A 38–40°C bath will raise your core body temperature; the subsequent cooling triggers melatonin production and natural drowsiness. Aim for 15–20 minutes in the main bath. Pair with a light meal afterward — ramen near the sento is a classic move.

Day 1 — Can't Sleep (02:00–05:00)

Late-Night Onsen Rescue

Wide awake at 2 AM? That's jet lag. Head to a 24-hour super sento like Thermae-Yu in Shinjuku or Spa LaQua in Bunkyo. The combination of hot baths and cool-down rest areas creates a powerful sleep pressure effect. Many offer reclining chairs where you can rest (仮眠) until morning trains start at 5 AM.

Day 2 — Morning (06:00–09:00)

Cold Plunge Wake-Up

Start day two with a cold water bath (水風呂, mizuburo) alternated with hot. The temperature contrast shocks your system awake, boosts cortisol (good in the morning), and resets your circadian rhythm. Many sento open at 6 AM — perfect for an early recalibration session. Follow with a Japanese breakfast.

Day 2 — Afternoon (14:00–16:00)

Avoid the Afternoon Crash

The dreaded 2 PM slump hits hard with jet lag. Instead of napping (which would reset your clock wrong), try a brief 10-minute sauna session followed by a cool shower. The endorphin rush and skin stimulation will carry you through to evening. Find a sento with a good sauna — we've tagged them all in this guide.

Day 3 — Evening Routine (20:00–22:00)

Establish the Ritual

By day three, you should be locking in a consistent bathing routine. Visit a sento at the same time each evening — this ritual signals to your body that sleep time approaches. The Japanese concept of "湯治" (toji, bathing therapy) has centuries of wisdom behind it. Gradually your body adjusts, and the sento becomes your Tokyo rhythm anchor.

Pro Tips

Science-Backed Bathing Protocol

Research shows bathing 1–2 hours before sleep at 40–42.5°C for 10+ minutes can improve sleep onset by 36%. Combine with bright light exposure during Tokyo daytime, avoid screens after your evening bath, and drink the post-bath milk. Your body will thank you — and your jet lag will vanish days faster than without the hot soak protocol.
