// 01 — Rail Network

JR East — The Backbone

JR East (East Japan Railway Company) operates the most iconic lines in Tokyo, including the Yamanote Loop, plus all Shinkansen connections. If you only learn one system, learn this one.

🟢 Yamanote Line — The Loop

The circular lifeline of Tokyo, connecting all major stations in a 34.5 km loop. Full circuit takes approximately 60 minutes. Trains run every 2–4 minutes during rush hour.

Key Stations

  • Tokyo (東京) — Central hub, Shinkansen terminal, Marunouchi business district
  • Shibuya (渋谷) — Scramble crossing, shopping, nightlife hub
  • Shinjuku (新宿) — World's busiest station (3.6M daily), west-side skyscrapers, Kabukicho
  • Ikebukuro (池袋) — Anime culture, Sunshine City, department stores
  • Ueno (上野) — Museums, Ameyoko market, zoo, park
  • Akihabara (秋葉原) — Electronics, anime, maid cafes
  • Shinagawa (品川) — Shinkansen stop, gateway to Haneda
  • Harajuku (原宿) — Takeshita Street, Meiji Shrine, youth fashion
  • Ebisu (恵比寿) — Beer museum, upscale dining, garden place

🟠 Chuo Line (Rapid) — East-West Express

The orange express line slicing east-west across the city from Tokyo Station to the western suburbs. Rapid service skips smaller stations, making it faster than the Chuo-Sobu Local.

Key Stops

  • Tokyo → Ochanomizu — University district, instrument shops
  • Yotsuya — Sophia University area, upscale residential
  • Shinjuku — Major transfer hub
  • Nakano — Broadway (otaku haven), affordable eats
  • Kichijoji — Inokashira Park, Harmonica Yokocho, consistently voted "most wanted neighborhood"
  • Tachikawa — Showa Kinen Park, gateway to Mt. Takao

🟡 Chuo-Sobu Line (Local) — The Slow Ride

The yellow local line stopping at every station between Mitaka and Chiba. Runs on the same tracks as Chuo Rapid in central Tokyo but stops at all stations. Best for stations that Rapid skips: Iidabashi, Suidobashi, Akihabara, Ryogoku.

🔵 Keihin-Tohoku & Negishi Line

Blue line running parallel to Yamanote between Tabata and Shinagawa, then continuing south to Yokohama. During daytime, rapid service skips some stations. Useful for Ofuna, Kawasaki, and avoiding the often-packed Yamanote.

🚅 Shinkansen (Bullet Trains)

Departing from Tokyo or Shinagawa stations, the Shinkansen connects Tokyo to the rest of Japan at speeds up to 320 km/h. Not covered by regular Tokyo tickets — requires separate fare + reserved/unreserved seat tickets.

Important

The Japan Rail Pass covers all Shinkansen lines except the Nozomi and Mizuho services on the Tokaido/Sanyo line. Use Hikari or Kodama instead. Always reserve seats during peak seasons (Golden Week, Obon, New Year).

Other JR Lines in Tokyo

Saikyo Line / Rinkai Line — Shibuya to Saitama & Odaiba

Runs from Osaki through Shibuya, Shinjuku, Ikebukuro, and into Saitama. Through-service with the Rinkai Line lets you reach Odaiba (Tokyo Teleport station). Color: dark green. Infamous for extreme rush-hour crowding on the Ikebukuro–Shinjuku section (199% capacity in peak).

Keiyo Line — Tokyo Disney & Makuhari

Runs from Tokyo Station (underground platform, long walk from main concourse) to Maihama (Tokyo Disney Resort) and Kaihin-Makuhari. Color: wine red. The underground platform is 500m from the main station — allow 10 extra minutes.

Nambu Line — Kawasaki to Tachikawa

North-south line in the western suburbs connecting Kawasaki to Tachikawa via Musashi-Kosugi and Noborito. Useful for reaching suburbs not on main lines.

Yokosuka Line & Shonan-Shinjuku Line

Yokosuka Line: Tokyo to Kamakura/Yokosuka, often coupled with Sobu Rapid. Shonan-Shinjuku Line: connects Shinjuku directly to Yokohama, Kamakura, and Odawara without going through Tokyo Station. Very convenient for day trips.

Musashino Line — Suburban Connector

Arc-shaped line connecting the suburbs, useful for bypassing central Tokyo when traveling between outer areas (Nishi-Funabashi to Fuchu-Honmachi). Also serves Maihama for Disney access from the north.

First trains depart around 05:00. Last trains depart between 00:00 and 00:30 depending on the line and direction. There is NO 24-hour service on any metro line.

Last Train Warning

Metro last train times are NOT the same as JR. If your journey requires transferring between operators, always check the last connection time, not just the last departure on each individual line. Missing a single transfer can strand you.

// 03 — City-Operated Subway

Toei Subway — 4 Lines + Bonus Systems

Operated by the Tokyo Metropolitan Bureau of Transportation. Separate fare system from Tokyo Metro (no free transfers between the two without a pass), but IC cards work seamlessly. Generally less crowded than Metro equivalents.

Oedo Line — The Deep One

The Oedo Line is the deepest subway in Tokyo (up to 48.9m below street level at Roppongi). Its 6-shaped route covers areas other lines miss entirely — particularly useful for reaching Roppongi, Tsukiji Market area, and the Ryogoku sumo district.

Pro Tip

Oedo Line platforms are very deep. At Roppongi station, budget 5+ minutes just to get from street level to the platform. The elevators and long escalators are not fast.

Toei Bonus Systems

Toden Arakawa Line (Tokyo Sakura Tram)

Tokyo's last remaining tram line. Runs 12.2 km from Minowabashi to Waseda along quiet residential streets. A charming, slow ride through old-school Tokyo neighborhoods. Flat fare: ¥170 (IC: ¥168). Great for visiting Sugamo ("Grandma's Harajuku") and the rose gardens along the tracks.

Nippori-Toneri Liner

Automated guideway transit in the northern suburbs. Connects Nippori to Minumadai-Shinsuikouen. Elevated views, driverless operation. Useful primarily for locals — tourists rarely need it unless visiting the Toneri area.

// 04 — Private Railways (私鉄)

Private Railways — The Other Half

Japan's private railways (shitetsu) carry as many passengers as JR in the Tokyo area. Each company radiates outward from a major terminal station and often integrates with Metro lines via through-service (相互直通運転).

Odakyu Electric Railway

Terminal: Shinjuku → Odawara / Enoshima
  • Romancecar express to Hakone (85 min, ¥2,330)
  • Through-service with Chiyoda Line
  • Shimokitazawa — trendy neighborhood access
  • Day trips to Hakone & Enoshima

Keio Corporation

Terminal: Shinjuku → Hashimoto / Takaosanguchi
  • Best route to Mt. Takao (direct from Shinjuku)
  • Keio Line + Inokashira Line (to Kichijoji)
  • Budget-friendly alternative to JR for western suburbs
  • Through-service with Toei Shinjuku Line

Tokyu Corporation

Terminal: Shibuya / Meguro → Yokohama area
  • Toyoko Line: Shibuya → Yokohama (25 min, express)
  • Den-en-toshi Line: Shibuya → Chuo-Rinkan
  • Through-service with Fukutoshin/Hanzomon Lines
  • Access to Jiyugaoka, Futakotamagawa

Seibu Railway

Terminal: Ikebukuro / Shinjuku → Saitama
  • Ikebukuro Line + Shinjuku Line
  • Laview limited express to Chichibu (scenic area)
  • Toshimaen/Nerima/Tokorozawa access
  • Through-service with Yurakucho/Fukutoshin Lines

Tobu Railway

Terminal: Asakusa / Ikebukuro → Tochigi / Saitama
  • Spacia X limited express to Nikko (1h50m)
  • Skytree Line from Asakusa (Skytree access)
  • Tojo Line from Ikebukuro to Kawagoe
  • Through-service with Hanzomon/Hibiya Lines

Keikyu Corporation

Terminal: Shinagawa → Yokohama / Haneda Airport
  • Direct service to Haneda Airport (11 min from Shinagawa)
  • Through-service with Toei Asakusa Line
  • Alternative route to Yokohama
  • Airport Rapid Express — fast and frequent

Keisei Electric Railway

Terminal: Ueno → Narita Airport
  • Skyliner: Ueno → Narita in 36 min (¥2,520)
  • Access Express: cheaper, slightly slower
  • Through-service with Toei Asakusa Line
  • Primary Narita Airport rail connection

Tsukuba Express (TX)

Terminal: Akihabara → Tsukuba
  • Akihabara to Tsukuba in 45 min
  • Passes through Asakusa (separate station)
  • Relatively new (2005), modern stations
  • Good for Kashiwa-no-ha, Moriya areas
Through-Service Explained

Many private railways run continuous service onto Metro lines (and vice versa). A train that starts as a Tokyu Toyoko Line train may become a Fukutoshin Line train at Shibuya, then a Seibu Ikebukuro Line train at Kotake-mukaihara — all without you needing to change seats. However, fare is calculated separately for each operator. IC cards handle this automatically; paper tickets do not.

Taxi Tips

Doors open and close automatically — do NOT touch them. Tipping is not expected and may cause confusion. Show the driver your destination on Google Maps if there's a language barrier. The red light on the dashboard means vacant/available.

// 08 — Self-Drive

Rental Cars — When (Not) to Drive

Driving in central Tokyo is generally not recommended. However, a car becomes invaluable for rural day trips, Hokkaido road trips, or accessing remote areas that trains don't serve.

🚗 Should You Rent?

YES — Rent When

  • Visiting rural areas (Hokkaido, Shikoku, Kyushu)
  • Traveling with family/heavy luggage
  • Day trips to Mt. Fuji area, Izu Peninsula
  • Exploring the countryside at your own pace
  • Visiting multiple small towns in one day

NO — Don't Rent When

  • Staying within Tokyo 23 wards
  • Traveling between major cities (use Shinkansen)
  • You're unfamiliar with left-side driving
  • You don't have an International Driving Permit (IDP)
  • Budget is tight (tolls + parking = expensive)

Requirements

  • International Driving Permit (IDP) based on the 1949 Geneva Convention — must be obtained BEFORE arriving in Japan
  • Your original home country driver's license (carry both)
  • Minimum age: 18 (some companies require 21 for certain classes)
  • Credit card for deposit

Expressway Tolls

Tokyo expressway tolls are expensive. A trip from Tokyo to Hakone on the Tomei Expressway costs approximately ¥2,500 one way. Rent an ETC card from the rental company for automated toll payment (¥330/day). Without ETC, you'll queue at manual toll booths.

// 09 — Two Wheels

Cycling — Tokyo by Bike

Flat terrain, excellent infrastructure in some wards, and shared bike stations everywhere make cycling a surprisingly good way to cover short-to-medium distances. Many locals commute this way.

🚲 Bike Share Services

¥165 / 30 min (electric)
Largest network, 1,100+ ports in 23 wards
  • Electric-assist bikes (red frames)
  • One-day pass: ¥1,650 (unlimited 30-min rides)

Hello Cycling

¥70 / 15 min

Cycling Rules in Japan

Ride on the left side of the road (same as cars). Cycling on sidewalks is technically illegal in most areas but widely tolerated when marked. No cycling while holding an umbrella, using a phone, or wearing earbuds (fines up to ¥50,000). Bicycle helmets became a legal "effort obligation" for all riders in April 2023.

// 12 — Tap & Ride

Suica & Pasmo — Your Essential Cards

Rechargeable IC (integrated circuit) cards that work on virtually all trains, buses, monorails, and even convenience stores and vending machines across Japan. Get one immediately upon arrival — it's the single most useful thing you can do.

In practice, Suica and Pasmo are completely interchangeable. Pick whichever you encounter first.

How to Get an IC Card

Option 1: Physical Card (Limited Availability)

Due to a global semiconductor shortage, physical Suica/Pasmo cards have been limited since 2023. As of 2025, availability has improved but is still inconsistent. Welcome Suica cards (28-day expiry, no deposit) are sold at Narita/Haneda airports.

  • JR ticket machines (green machines) — look for English option
  • Airport JR East Travel Service Centers
  • Some convenience stores near major stations

Option 2: Mobile Suica / Pasmo (Recommended)

The best option for tourists. Available on iPhone (Wallet app) and Android (Google Pay). No physical card needed, no semiconductor shortage issues.

  • iPhone: Settings → Wallet → Add Card → Transit Card → Suica or Pasmo
  • Android: Google Pay → Transit → Add Suica
  • Charge with any international credit/debit card
  • Express Transit mode: no Face ID/fingerprint needed — just tap the reader
Mobile Suica Setup Tip

Set up Mobile Suica BEFORE you land in Japan. You can add it to your phone from anywhere in the world and charge it with your home credit card. When you arrive, you're ready to tap through gates immediately. Initial charge minimum: ¥1,000.

Charging Your Card

  • Ticket Machines: Insert your card, select "charge," insert cash. Accepts ¥1,000, ¥2,000, ¥5,000, ¥10,000 notes
  • Convenience Stores: Tell the clerk "charge onegaishimasu" and hand them your card + cash
  • Mobile: Charge via credit card in the app (no cash needed)
  • Maximum Balance: ¥20,000

Critical Warning

The "last train" shown on timetables is the last departure from the ORIGIN station. Your station may be mid-line, meaning the last train passing through could be 10–30 minutes later. Always search your SPECIFIC station, not just the line. Use Google Maps' "Depart At" feature set to midnight to check.

Missed the Last Train? Your Options:

1. Taxi (Most Common)

Flag one down or use the GO app. Expect 20% late-night surcharge (22:00–05:00). A ride from Shinjuku to Shibuya is only ~¥1,500 even late at night. Split with friends to make it affordable. Taxi queues at major stations can be 30+ minutes after midnight on weekends.

2. Manga Cafe / Internet Cafe (漫画喫茶)

The classic last-train-miss solution. Private booths with reclining seats, free drinks, showers, and manga/internet access. Chains include Manboo!, Popeye, and Gran Cyber Cafe. Night packs (6–8 hours) typically run ¥1,500–¥2,500.

3. Capsule Hotel

Small sleeping pods clustered near major stations. Clean, quiet, and surprisingly comfortable. Expect ¥3,000–¥5,000 for a night. Book via apps like Booking.com or just walk in — most have availability on weeknights. Nine Hours, First Cabin, and Anshin Oyado are popular chains.

4. Karaoke All-Night

Karaoke chains offer all-night packages (フリータイム) starting around ¥1,500–¥2,500 with unlimited drinks. Not the best sleep, but fun with friends. Big Echo, Karaoke Kan, and Joysound are everywhere.

5. 24-Hour Restaurants

Denny's, Jonathan's, Gusto, Saizeriya — Japan's family restaurants are open 24/7 in many locations. A ¥500 order buys you a warm seat until the first train at 5 AM. Technically not for sleeping, but nobody will bother you if you're nursing a coffee.

6. Sento / Onsen

Some bathhouses offer late-night / overnight stays. Thermae-Yu in Shinjuku (open until 9 AM) is a popular option with full bathing facilities and rest areas. Entry around ¥2,600.

7. Walk It

Tokyo is extremely safe at all hours. If your hotel is within 5 km, a 45-60 minute walk through quiet nighttime streets can be a memorable experience. Use Google Maps walking mode.

// 18 — Know the Rules

Etiquette, Accessibility & Pro Tips

Knowing the unwritten rules will make your experience smoother and earn you respect from locals.

🙇 Train Etiquette

  • Queue up: Lines form on platform markings. Wait for passengers to exit before boarding.
  • No phone calls: Set your phone to manner mode (マナーモード). Texting is fine; talking is not.
  • No eating/drinking: On commuter trains, eating and drinking is frowned upon. Long-distance trains (Shinkansen, limited express) are fine.
  • Backpacks: Hold your backpack in front of you or place it on the overhead rack. Never leave it on your back in a crowded train.
  • Priority seats: 優先席 (yūsenseki) are for elderly, pregnant, disabled, and injured passengers. Offer your seat even from regular seats if someone needs it.
  • Women-only cars: 女性専用車両 are usually the first or last car during morning rush. Men should avoid them during posted hours.
  • Keep left: On escalators, stand on the left in Tokyo (opposite to Osaka's right-side standing). Walking on the right side is being phased out, but old habits persist.
  • Volume: Speak quietly. Trains in Tokyo are remarkably silent during commute hours.

♿ Accessibility

Tokyo's rail system has made significant accessibility improvements:

  • Most stations have at least one barrier-free route (elevator + flat path to platform)
  • Tactile paving (yellow bumps) guides visually impaired passengers on all platforms
  • Station staff will deploy ramps for wheelchair users — press the intercom at the gate
  • Priority elevators are available but often crowded — budget extra time
  • Newer trains (Yamanote E235, etc.) have dedicated wheelchair spaces
  • The Namboku Line has platform screen doors at every station for safety
  • Audio announcements in Japanese and English on most lines
Wheelchair Users

Contact your departing station in advance (or use the intercom at the gate). Staff will coordinate with the arrival station to have someone waiting with a ramp. This service is free and well-practiced. Japan Accessible Tourism Center (accessible-japan.com) has detailed station-by-station accessibility information.

🧳 Luggage Tips

  • Coin lockers: Available at all major stations (¥400–¥800 per day). Large sizes fill up by mid-morning at popular stations — arrive early or use Ecbo Cloak luggage storage service.
  • Luggage forwarding (Takkyubin): Ship your suitcase ahead via Yamato Transport or Sagawa Express from your hotel to the airport or next hotel. ¥2,000–¥3,000 per bag, next-day delivery. Absolute game-changer for travel days.
  • On trains: Large suitcases are awkward on commuter trains during rush hour. Shinkansen have overhead racks and behind-seat storage (reserve "extra-large luggage" seats if your bag exceeds 160cm total dimensions).
  • Avoid rush hour with luggage: If carrying bags, travel before 7 AM or after 10 AM. You'll be miserable — and making others miserable — on a packed rush-hour train with a suitcase.

💡 Power User Tips

  • Station numbering: Every station has a letter-number code (e.g., G-01 for Shibuya on Ginza Line). Use these when asking for directions — clearer than pronunciation.
  • Exit numbers matter: Large stations have 20+ exits. Google Maps shows the specific exit number closest to your destination. Using the right exit saves 5–10 min.
  • Transfer walks: "Transfer" doesn't mean stepping off one train onto another. At major stations (Shinjuku, Tokyo, Otemachi), transfers can involve 5–15 minute walks through underground passages. Budget accordingly.
  • Ekiben: Station bento boxes (駅弁) are sold at major stations, especially for Shinkansen travel. Each station has unique regional specialties.
  • Rush hour hack: Trains are less crowded toward the ends of the platform. If you must ride during rush hour, walk to the first or last car.
  • Free Wi-Fi: Tokyo Metro and Toei offer free Wi-Fi at stations. JR East has JR-EAST FREE Wi-Fi. Quality is spotty — don't rely on it.
  • Lost property: If you leave something on a train, go to the station's lost-and-found (落とし物センター) or call the operator. Japanese people routinely turn in lost items — your chances of recovery are extremely high.
